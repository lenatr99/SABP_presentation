Statistical analysis of single-cell RNA-seq in Dictyostelium development

Worked example: quantifying developmental synchronicity — from raw cells to replicate-level hypothesis tests.

Lena Trnovec
1. Biological system

Why Dictyostelium?

02 / 06

A timed developmental program with matched mutant controls — a clean setup for statistics.

  • Starvation triggers a ~20-hour developmental program; sampling at fixed intervals gives temporal snapshots.
  • cAMP pulses coordinate aggregation; later cells split into prespore and prestalk fates.
  • Two mutants disrupt cAMP signaling — one blocks development entirely, one develops without pulses.
  • Matched genetic perturbation gives a mechanistically motivated control group, not just a larger sample.
02 / 06
2. Dataset

Single-cell time course

03 / 06

Gene-expression matrix plus replication structure — the statistics depend on both.

  • ~50,000 cells total; rows = cells, columns = genes, entries = transcript counts.
  • Sampled at 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20 h after starvation, for three strains (AX4, acaA⁻, acaA⁻pkaCOE).
  • Three to four independent biological replicates per strain × time point — separate cultures, each tagged with a distinct fluorescent protein (GFP / mCerulean / mCherry / mNeonGreen) and pooled only at sequencing.
  • 50,000 cells ≠ 50,000 independent observations — the replicate count is what carries the inference (see slide 5).

3. The pattern in the data

What the data show: UMAPs

04 / 06

Each dot is one cell; similar cells sit close together on the map.

  • Wild-type by time: two clusters — early (0–4 h) and later stages — with a clean temporal path between them.
  • Wild-type by cell type: the later cluster splits into two fates, prespore and prestalk.
  • acaA⁻ (no cAMP signaling): cells are stuck in the early region — development never starts.
  • acaA⁻pkaCOE (develops without cAMP pulses): cells do reach the later stages, but time points are mixed — development happens, but without synchronicity.
  • Next: how we turn this visual pattern into a replicate-level statistical test.

4. Statistical reasoning

Hypotheses, assumptions, choice of test

05 / 06
Worked example: AX4 vs acaA⁻ at 8 h H₀: mean synchronicity equal. Hₐ: strains differ — cAMP signaling matters.
1. Independence Cells from one culture are not independent — treating them so is pseudo-replication. Aggregate to one value per biological replicate → n = 3, not 5,000.
2. 95% CI by non-parametric bootstrap σ unknown (no z-interval); 3 replicates ⟹ normality unverifiable (t-interval risky). Resample 10,000× ; take 2.5th–97.5th percentiles.
3. Replicate-level t-test (Welch) Continuous outcome (not χ²); pairwise comparison (ANOVA not needed); σ unknown (not z). Welch handles unequal variances.

5. Where this is heading

Predicting transcription factors with AI

06 / 06

Once synchronicity is established, the next question is which transcription factors drive the split into prespore and prestalk fates.

  • Use AI models — foundation models trained on large single-cell collections, together with network-based approaches — to predict candidate transcription factors.
  • Models bring in biological context that a single dataset cannot provide on its own.
  • Output is a prioritized shortlist of hypotheses, not a definitive list of hits — the lab is what turns a candidate into a finding.
  • Early stages — open to suggestions on models, methods, and pitfalls to watch for.
